\paragraph{Common Protocol for the Availability-Mismatch Experiments}
In the two experiments that follow, participation is availability-driven: in
each round the server draws an active set $S^t$ of $K$ users without
replacement with probabilities proportional to per-user availability weights
$r$, which induces the availability distribution $q$ over the
$M=\binom{N}{K}$ participating sets $\mathcal{A}_j$; we estimate $q$ from
$10^6$ Monte Carlo draws of this sampler ($4\times10^5$ in the synthetic
study) and fit the plan $T$ by Sinkhorn scaling under the membership mask,
normalizing each column to the FedAVOT weights $Y[i,j]=T[i,j]/q_j$. Every
active user performs $H=5$ local epochs of full-batch gradient descent, and
FedAVOT, FedAvg with $K$ users, and full-participation FedAvg are updated in
parallel from the same draw $S^t$, so their trajectories are directly
comparable. We report the importance-weighted objective $F(\theta^t)$ under
the squared loss per communication round; final values average the last
$500$ rounds and are given as mean $\pm$ standard deviation over seeds.
Recall that the transport is feasible precisely when
$p_i \le \pi_i := \sum_{j:\, i \in \mathcal{A}_j} q_j$ for every user $i$;
we quantify infeasibility as the importance mass
$\sum_{i:\, p_i > \pi_i} p_i$ held by violating users, and design $r$ to
place each experiment on a chosen side of this condition.

\paragraph{IMDb-Wiki Age Regression Under Availability--Importance Mismatch}
We group IMDb-Wiki images by celebrity identity and take the first $N=100$
identities with at least $30$ training images as users, each holding $30$
images represented by $128$-dimensional face embeddings from a pretrained
ResNet; the task is linear regression of mean-centered age on the
embedding, with $K=3$, learning rate $\eta_\theta=10^{-2}$, $4000$
communication rounds ($5000$ in the feasible variant), and $5$ seeds. Both
availability regimes share the same cubic importance
$p_i \propto (N{+}1{-}i)^3$ and differ only in $r$. In the
\emph{mirrored} regime, $r_i \propto i^3$ is the exact mirror image of
$p$, so the most important users are almost never available: $41$ of
$100$ users violate the feasibility condition, and they hold
approximately $88\%$ of the importance mass. In the \emph{aligned} regime,
$r_i \propto N{+}1{-}i$ is a linear skew in the same direction as $p$;
feasibility then holds for every user ($\max_i p_i/\pi_i = 0.66$), while
the cubic-versus-linear shape mismatch still requires nontrivial transport
reweighting.

\paragraph{Controlled Synthetic Study of the Feasibility Boundary}
To isolate the effect of feasibility we use a synthetic linear-regression
setting in which the skew of both distributions is a single knob $\alpha$:
$p_i \propto (N{+}1{-}i)^{\alpha}$ and $r_i \propto i^{\alpha}$, with
$N=50$ and $K=3$. User $i$ holds $30$ standard-normal samples in $d=40$
dimensions, and labels follow a user-specific model
$\theta_i^{\star} = \bar\theta + h\,\delta_i u + 0.3\,\varepsilon_i$ with
label-noise standard deviation $0.1$, where $\delta_i = 2i/(N{-}1)-1$
orders the heterogeneity along the availability axis and $h=3$ sets its
strength; the distribution shift is thus availability-correlated, so which
users participate determines which optimum is reached. We train with
$\eta_\theta = 10^{-3}$ for $1500$ communication rounds ($5000$ in the
extended feasible run) over $3$ seeds, sweeping
$\alpha \in \{0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 3\}$, which spans roughly $14\%$ to $87\%$
infeasible importance mass; the feasibility-mechanism diagnostic contrasts
$\alpha = 0.2$ (fully feasible) with $\alpha = 3.0$.
